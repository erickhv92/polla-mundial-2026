## Scouting Summary

Tunisia arrive at the 2026 World Cup as one of the most defensively disciplined sides in the field, having completed a near-flawless qualifying campaign (9 wins, 1 draw, zero goals conceded across 10 matches). Under new coach Sabri Lamouchi, they are organized, hard-working and difficult to break down, but their persistent flaw is a lack of a genuine, proven international goalscorer. Drawn into a tough Group F with the Netherlands, Japan and Sweden, Tunisia will likely look to grind out low-scoring games and steal results on set pieces and counters.

## Tactics

Lamouchi has built Tunisia around defensive solidity and midfield control. Expect a compact block, with the midfield packed by duel-winners and runners (Skhiri, Khedira, Ben Slimane) to combat the superior technical quality of group opponents. They look to win the ball, dictate tempo through Skhiri and Mejbri, and spring attacks via wide runners (Achouri, Tounekti). There is talk of shifting to a five-at-the-back system to add stability against the Netherlands and Sweden. The base shape is a 4-3-3 / 4-4-2.

## Strengths

- **Elite defensive organization** — unbeaten with zero goals conceded across 10 qualifiers.
- **Reliable, settled backline** (Talbi, Bronn) and a strong goalkeeper.
- **Midfield stability and steel** — Skhiri and Mejbri offer Champions League / Premier League pedigree and ball-winning ability.
- **Set-piece threat** — multiple goals from defenders/midfielders in qualifying.
- **Tournament experience and resilience** — third consecutive World Cup.

## Weaknesses

- **No proven international goalscorer** — top scorer is a defender (Ali Abdi, 8). Limited cutting edge in the final third.
- **Struggles to break down deep blocks** and against superior opponents (poor 2025 AFCON, eliminated in last 16).
- **Quality gap** vs. Group F rivals is significant compared with their qualifying opposition.
- **Controversial squad omissions** weaken attacking depth (see below).

## Verdict

Tunisia are a textbook tournament dark-horse-on-defense: tough to beat, well-drilled, and led by quality midfielders in Skhiri and Mejbri. But in a brutal Group F, their lack of a reliable striker and limited attacking ceiling make scoring goals the central question. Realistic ceiling is to nick a result and fight for second/third place; most likely they grind out tight games but bow out at the group stage unless Mejbri and the wide men find an extra gear. **Outsiders to advance, near-zero title hopes (~500/1).**
