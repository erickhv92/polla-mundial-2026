## Scouting Summary

Ghana arrive at the 2026 World Cup as a talent-rich but unstable side, having sacked Otto Addo just 72 days before the tournament and handed the reins to veteran World Cup specialist Carlos Queiroz. The Black Stars qualified comfortably from CAF but then collapsed in friendlies, losing five straight before a morale-restoring 1-1 draw with Wales. Robbed of their two best attackers (Mohammed Kudus and Mohammed Salisu) to injury and fielding 15 World Cup debutants, Ghana are a coin-flip to escape a group also containing England.

## Tactics

Queiroz is expected to impose a disciplined, rigid defensive structure — a clear break from Addo's situational adaptability. Look for a compact mid-block in either a 4-2-3-1 or a 3-4-2-1, with Partey shielding the back line and Ghana looking to spring fast, direct transitions through the pace of Semenyo, Williams, Kamaldeen and Fatawu. The second-half Wales performance hinted at more tactical clarity and dynamism than the friendly losses suggested. With only weeks of preparation, expect pragmatism over fluency.

---

## Strengths

- Elite, in-form wide attackers (Semenyo, Williams, Kamaldeen, Fatawu) with genuine pace and dribbling.
- Antoine Semenyo is a top-tier Premier League goal threat at the peak of his form.
- Tournament pedigree and tactical know-how in Carlos Queiroz (managed at four World Cups).
- Thomas Partey provides a high-quality, experienced midfield anchor.
- Dangerous on the counter-attack with direct transition runners.

---

## Weaknesses

- Lost two best players to injury: Mohammed Kudus (quad surgery) and Mohammed Salisu (ACL).
- Severe instability: coach changed 72 days out; 15 debutants and minimal time to gel.
- Recurring defensive fragility, especially conceding late goals (Wales equaliser).
- Poor recent scoring output — only 2 goals in roughly 6 pre-tournament matches.
- Unsettled, relatively untested defence and goalkeeping behind the stars.

## Verdict

Ghana are a boom-or-bust side. The attacking firepower on the wings — led by an in-form Antoine Semenyo — is good enough to trouble anyone on a counter-attacking day, and Queiroz's tournament experience is a stabilising asset. But losing Kudus and Salisu, the coaching upheaval, defensive frailty and dire pre-tournament form make consistency a real concern. Realistic ceiling: second in Group L and a Round of 32 / Round of 16 run. Realistic floor: a chaotic group-stage exit. Lean: scrap into the knockouts as group runners-up behind England, then vulnerable. Not a title contender at ~250/1.
